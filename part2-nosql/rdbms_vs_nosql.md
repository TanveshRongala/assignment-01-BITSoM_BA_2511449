## Database Recommendation

For a patient management system, I would recommend **MySQL** as the primary database. Healthcare data is highly sensitive and operationally critical, so strong consistency and transaction reliability matter more than schema flexibility. Patient registration, appointment booking, prescriptions, billing, and medical records all require **ACID** properties: atomicity ensures that related updates either all succeed or all fail, consistency protects data integrity, isolation prevents concurrent transactions from interfering with each other, and durability guarantees that confirmed records are not lost. In a healthcare setting, these are not just technical preferences but business and safety requirements.

MongoDB, which is often associated with a more **BASE-oriented** model, is useful when applications need flexible schemas, rapid scaling, and high availability. However, for a core patient management system, eventual consistency can be risky. If one part of the system shows outdated patient or treatment information, that could lead to serious operational errors. From a **CAP theorem** perspective, a patient system should lean toward **consistency and partition tolerance** rather than availability at all costs, because accurate medical and billing data is more important than temporarily serving slightly stale information.

That said, my answer would partly change if the startup also needed a **fraud detection module**. Fraud detection often involves ingesting large volumes of semi-structured or fast-changing data such as logs, claim patterns, user activity, and anomaly indicators. In that case, MongoDB could be valuable as a **secondary database** for analytics-oriented or flexible data workloads. So I would still recommend **MySQL for the system of record**, but I would consider adding **MongoDB for fraud detection or event-driven analysis**. In other words, the best solution may be hybrid: relational for correctness, and NoSQL for flexible large-scale pattern analysis.
